Scene 1: The Hallway Conversation

Jordan has something to tell Marcus.

It is Tuesday morning. Marcus is on his third cup of coffee and his second attempt to find a parking spot. Jordan catches him just inside the building entrance.
🧑‍💻
Jordan
Hey Marcus, do you have a minute? I've been dealing with some health stuff lately. My doctor thinks I might need some changes to how I'm working. I'm not sure what that looks like yet, but I wanted to give you a heads up.
👨‍💼
Marcus
Oh. Okay. Thanks for letting me know. Is it… serious? Like, what kind of health stuff are we talking about?
🧑‍💻
Jordan
I'd rather not get into the details. I just know I might need some flexibility with my schedule or my workspace.
⚠️ Notice anything?
Jordan did not use the words "ADA," "disability," or "accommodation." That does not matter. A request for a change at work related to a medical condition is enough to trigger your organization's obligation to respond.

🚫 What went wrong?
Marcus made two classic mistakes: he probed for a diagnosis (which he is not entitled to), and he tried to evaluate the request himself. Supervisors are not authorized to conduct the interactive process. That is the Coordinator's role.
Know the Rules

What supervisors should and should not do.

✅ When an employee discloses a need, supervisors should:

  • 🤝
    Express support and thank the employee for sharing.
  • 📞
    Connect the employee to your organization's ADA/Accommodations Coordinator promptly.
  • 🔒
    Keep the conversation confidential. This information is not for team meetings or hallway chats.
  • 📋
    Cooperate with the Coordinator when asked for information about job duties or the work environment.

🚫 Supervisors should NOT:

  • 🩺
    Ask for a diagnosis or probe for medical details beyond what is relevant to the request.
  • ⚖️
    Make their own determination about whether someone qualifies for an accommodation.
  • 🛠️
    Implement informal "fixes" without going through the interactive process.
  • 🗣️
    Share the employee's health information with other staff or managers.
  • ⏳
    Delay. Unreasonable delays can themselves be a legal violation.

The Process

How the interactive process works.

The interactive process is a structured, good-faith conversation between the organization and the employee to identify an effective accommodation. Here is how it flows.
1

Disclosure / Request

Employee communicates a need for a change at work related to a health condition. No magic words required.

2

Supervisor Refers

Supervisor connects the employee to the ADA/Accommodations Coordinator without delay or interrogation.

3

Coordinator Engages

Coordinator meets with the employee, gathers documentation as needed, and reviews job functions with the supervisor.

4

Accommodation Identified

The organization and employee identify an effective accommodation. It does not have to be the employee's preferred option. It must be effective.

5

Implementation & Follow-Up

Accommodation is put in place. The Coordinator follows up to confirm it is working and adjusts if circumstances change.

🔑 The Big Three

  • 1️⃣
    Refer, do not resolve. When an employee discloses a need, your job is to connect them to the Accommodations Coordinator. Do not evaluate, diagnose, or fix it yourself.
  • 2️⃣
    You do not need a diagnosis. A request for a change at work related to a health condition is enough. The magic words do not matter.
  • 3️⃣
    Confidentiality is not optional. Medical information stays between you, the employee, and the Coordinator. It does not go to the team.
